What is Autopiot Embedded System?

Autopilot is a complex algotrithm that enables an aircraft to fly on its own, without pilots having to give the inputs for operation of flight. This phenomenen is knows as the Automatic Flight Control System which is embedded in most of the commercial jets.

How does Autopilot impact our flight safety?

On a long distance flight of upto 14 hours, only a maximun of 3 pilots are allowed. And only 2 have hands on the control deck of the cockpit. This would result in fatigue, physically and mentally in pilots. But not anymore! Since the Autopilot takes over most of the flight time, pilots have lower stress level, which results in improved efficiency.

This would rule out the possiblity of crash due to piloting error to a very high extent. The curical part of a flight, landing, is also taken care by autopilot these days, which also would rule out crash landing significantly.

How has Autopilot affected commercial jet business?

As the result of advanced technological progression, there are many airlines and airways companies being established, for business and flight experience improvement.

For a recent example, for the first time in Nepal, there is a new private airline company, Himalaya Airlines which has been established as a result of advances in automation and aviation.

There are more than 30,000 commercial jets with autopilot embedded system.

What Autopilot cannot do?

In commerical jets, autopilot cannot let the aircraft take off on its own. It also cannot initiaite communication and respond to air traffic controllers. It cannot ignite the engine neither it can turn of the aircraft on its own.

Could Autopilot malfunction?

Short answer, yes. There could be problems in the intruments, control system or error in pilot's input itself. Autopilot was invented just to provide a helping edge, not take full control of the flight.


Terms
Meanings

Actuators

 A type of motor that is responsible for moving or controlling a mechanism or system. It is operated by a source of energy, typically electric current, hydraulic fluid pressure, or pneumatic pressure, and converts that energy into motion.

Avionics

 Electronics as applied to aviation.

Cockpit

 Compartment for the pilot, and sometimes also the crew, in an aircraft or spacecraft.

Control Yoke

 A device used for controlling the attitude of the plane, usually in both pitch and roll.

Flux Valves

 A magnetic sensory device that electrically transmits information to an aircraft's compass system regarding its orientation relative to Earth's magnetic field.

Glideslope

 A ground device that uses lights to assist a pilot in landing an airplane at an airport.

Gyrocompass

 Nonmagnetic compass in which the direction of true north is maintained by a continuously driven gyroscope whose axis is parallel to the earth's axis of rotation.

Gyroscope

 Spinning wheel or disc in which the axis of rotation is free to assume any orientation by itself.

HSI

 The Horizontal Situation Indicator (commonly called the HSI) is an aircraft instrument normally mounted below the artificial horizon in place of a conventional heading indicator.

Localizer

 The lateral component of the instrument landing system (ILS) for the runway centreline.

Magneto

 A small electric generator containing a permanent magnet and used to provide high-voltage pulses, especially (formerly) in the ignition systems of internal combustion engines.

Omnidirectional

 Receiving signals from or transmitting in all directions.

Throttle

 A device controlling the flow of fuel or power to an engine.

Turbulence

 Violent or unsteady movement of air or water, or of some other fluid.

Vigilance

 Action or state of keeping careful watch for possible danger or difficulties.

Yaw

 A twisting or oscillation of a moving ship or aircraft around a vertical axis.
